A "full set" price posted on a salon's menu is almost always a starting point rather than the final total, and understanding why can help avoid surprises at checkout.

**Length and shape add cost.** Most salons charge a base price for a short-to-medium set, with an upcharge for extra length (like extended almond or coffin shapes) or for extra structural work like a sculpted tip versus a pre-formed one.

**Nail art is priced separately, often per nail.** A simple accent nail might be a flat few dollars, while detailed hand-painted art, 3D elements, or embedded charms are commonly priced per nail and can add significantly to the total on a full set of ten.

**Removal of a previous set isn't always included.** If you're coming in with an existing gel, dip, or acrylic set from another salon, expect a separate removal charge, especially if it takes extra time to soak off safely.

**Location and salon tier matter.** Prices for the same basic service can vary widely between a walk-in strip-mall salon and a boutique studio, largely reflecting rent, product quality, and appointment-based versus walk-in models.

**Ask for a full estimate before you sit down.** The most reliable way to avoid a surprise total is to describe exactly what you want — length, shape, and any art — before the service starts, so the technician can quote the real price rather than just the base rate.
