Many nail salons operate on a booth-rental model, where technicians rent chair space and run their own book of business as independent contractors rather than salon employees. Done correctly, it's a legitimate and common structure — but getting the classification wrong is one of the most common compliance issues in the industry.

**The distinction comes down to control, not labels.** Calling someone a "contractor" on paper doesn't make them one under tax law. If a salon sets a technician's hours, dictates which products they must use, and controls how they perform the service, tax authorities may consider that person an employee regardless of the paperwork.

**Misclassification shifts tax and insurance obligations.** When a technician is misclassified as a contractor, the salon avoids payroll tax withholding, workers' compensation premiums, and unemployment insurance contributions it would otherwise owe — which is exactly why regulators pay close attention to this specific issue in the industry.

**Booth rental agreements need to reflect real independence.** A properly structured booth-rental arrangement typically has the technician setting their own hours, prices, and client relationships, supplying their own tools, and paying a flat rent rather than a percentage tied to salon oversight.

**States are increasingly auditing this specifically.** A number of state labor and tax agencies have run targeted worker-classification audits in personal-care industries in recent years, given how common the booth-rental model is compared to other small-business sectors.

For salon owners, the safest path is making sure the actual day-to-day arrangement matches the legal definition of a contractor relationship — not just the label used in a lease agreement.
