With fewer new technicians entering the field each year, salon owners describe a shift from simply posting a job opening to actively competing on pay and perks to keep the chairs they already have staffed.

**Guaranteed minimums are becoming more common.** Rather than pure commission, some salons now offer a guaranteed hourly floor on slower days, reducing the income swings that used to push technicians toward other industries during quiet stretches.

**Booth rental terms are getting more flexible.** Salons that rent chairs to independent technicians are increasingly offering shorter lease commitments or tiered rent based on experience level, trying to make the arrangement more attractive to newly licensed workers.

**Continuing education is being offered in-house.** Advanced technique classes — intricate nail art, newer gel systems, or business skills for booth renters — are increasingly provided free or subsidized, both as a retention tool and a way to help technicians earn more per client.

**Health benefits remain rare but are appearing at larger chains.** Multi-location salon groups are more likely than independent shops to offer some form of health coverage or paid time off, which has become a meaningful differentiator in hiring.

None of this fully closes the gap yet, but it reflects a real shift: in a tighter labor market, technician retention has become a business priority rather than an afterthought.
