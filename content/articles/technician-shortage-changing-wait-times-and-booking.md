As staffing has tightened in many markets, the way clients book appointments has shifted along with it — often without much public discussion of why.

**Advance booking is replacing walk-in culture.** Salons that once ran almost entirely on walk-in traffic increasingly require appointments booked a week or more out, especially for popular technicians or weekend slots.

**Popular technicians are booking out further than the salon itself.** It's now common for a specific in-demand technician's calendar to be full for weeks even when the salon as a whole has same-day openings with other staff.

**Cancellation policies have gotten stricter.** With fewer chairs to fill, a last-minute no-show costs a salon more relative to its total capacity, which has pushed many shops toward deposits or cancellation fees that weren't common a few years ago.

**Some clients are shifting service types to fit availability.** Rather than wait for their preferred technician's full-set slot, some clients are opting for quicker services — a polish change or simple manicure — that fit into shorter, more available windows.

The shift isn't dramatic in every market, but in cities where the shortage is most acute, booking a nail appointment increasingly resembles booking any other in-demand personal service: plan ahead or take what's available.
