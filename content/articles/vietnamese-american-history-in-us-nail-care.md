Vietnamese Americans make up a significant share of licensed nail technicians in the United States today, and the roots of that connection go back to a specific, well-documented moment nearly fifty years ago.

**It started with a group of refugees in 1975.** After the fall of Saigon, actress Tippi Hedren, who volunteered with newly arrived Vietnamese refugees at Hope Village near Sacramento, noticed several women admiring her manicure. She arranged for her own manicurist to teach a small group basic nail care skills and helped connect graduates to jobs at local salons — a starting point widely cited as the origin of Vietnamese Americans' outsized presence in the industry.

**The trade offered something refugees needed at the time: accessible entry.** Compared to many professions, nail technician training required less English proficiency and a shorter, less expensive path to licensure, making it a practical way for new arrivals to build financial independence quickly.

**Community networks accelerated growth from there.** As early technicians became salon owners, many trained and employed newer arrivals and family members, and that pattern repeated across generations of immigration, spreading Vietnamese-owned salons into cities and towns nationwide.

**The industry today reflects that history directly.** Vietnamese-language trade publications, product suppliers catering specifically to Vietnamese-owned salons, and long-running community trade associations are all direct outgrowths of that original entry point in the 1970s.

It's a rare case where a single, specific act of assistance can be traced fairly directly to an entire industry's demographics decades later — and it's a piece of nail industry history that's often left out of conversations about where the trade is today.
