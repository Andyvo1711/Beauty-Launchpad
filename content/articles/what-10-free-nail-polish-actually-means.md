"5-free," "7-free," and now "10-free" have become common labels on polish bottles, but the terms aren't standardized the same way across every brand, which makes them worth understanding rather than taking at face value.

The original "5-free" claim referred to leaving out five ingredients that drew consumer concern in traditional nail polish formulas: formaldehyde, toluene, dibutyl phthalate (DBP), formaldehyde resin, and camphor. As demand for cleaner formulas grew, brands started adding more excluded ingredients to the list — things like xylene, ethyl tosylamide, and certain UV-reactive photoinitiators used in gel systems.

**More free-from claims don't automatically mean a gentler product.** Removing one ingredient sometimes means a formula relies more heavily on another to achieve the same wear time or finish, so "10-free" isn't a guarantee that a polish is hypoallergenic or irritation-free for everyone.

**Gel and dip systems have their own considerations.** Because these formulas cure under UV/LED light or build up in layers, questions about ingredient lists often come up alongside separate concerns about UV exposure during curing and file dust during removal.

**Ask your salon what they carry.** Many salons now stock at least one clean or low-chemical polish line alongside traditional options, so if ingredient sensitivity is a concern, it's reasonable to ask before your appointment rather than after a reaction shows up.
