For a small salon owner, a state sales tax audit notice can feel like a serious event, but in practice most audits follow a fairly standard, procedural path rather than an immediate accusation of wrongdoing.

**It usually starts with a records request, not a visit.** Auditors typically ask for a defined period of sales records, bank deposits, point-of-sale reports, and tax returns — often a year or two of history — rather than showing up unannounced.

**Auditors are checking for consistency, not perfection.** The core question is usually whether reported sales line up reasonably with bank deposits, purchase volume of retail products, and the number of service chairs or stations in operation — not whether every receipt is flawless.

**Cash businesses are asked to explain their reconciliation process.** Because cash isn't automatically documented the way card payments are, auditors commonly ask how a salon tracks and deposits cash revenue day to day, and gaps in that process are the most frequent source of follow-up questions.

**Most audits end in either a clean result or a proposed adjustment — not penalties.** If discrepancies are found, the typical outcome is a bill for additional tax owed plus interest; criminal referrals are reserved for cases involving clear intent to defraud, which is a small minority of audits.

**Representation is optional but common.** Many small business owners bring in an accountant or tax professional for the audit itself, mainly to make sure records are presented clearly rather than because the process is inherently adversarial.

The best preparation, according to most tax professionals, is simply keeping consistent daily records year-round — audits are far more stressful for businesses that have to reconstruct records after the fact than for those that already have them.
