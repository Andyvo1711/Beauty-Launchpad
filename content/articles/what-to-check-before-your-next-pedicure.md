Pedicures involve soaking feet in shared basins and working around cuticles and skin that's more prone to small cuts, which is why hygiene practices matter more here than for most other salon services.

**Ask about foot bath disinfection.** Reputable salons clean whirlpool or jetted foot basins after every single client, and do a deeper disinfection cycle at least once a day. If a salon can't describe their process when asked, that's worth noting.

**Look at the tool setup.** Metal tools should be sterilized in an autoclave or come from sealed, single-use packaging. Foot files, pumice stones, and buffer blocks ideally should be single-use or assigned to you for the visit rather than reused across clients.

**Skip it if you have a cut, rash, or infection.** Any open skin on the foot or lower leg is a reason to reschedule — a licensed technician should decline the service in this situation rather than working around it.

**Be cautious with aggressive callus removal.** Credo blades and overly aggressive callus shaving are restricted or banned in many states specifically because of infection risk; gentler exfoliation methods are the safer standard.

**Trust your instincts about cleanliness.** A visibly worn, unclean pedicure chair or foot basin is a reasonable reason to ask for a different station or reschedule.

None of this means pedicures are inherently risky when done at a well-run salon — it just means a few quick questions up front go a long way.
