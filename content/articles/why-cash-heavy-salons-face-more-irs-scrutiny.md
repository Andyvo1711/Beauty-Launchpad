Nail salons, along with restaurants, car washes, and other cash-heavy service businesses, tend to draw more tax audit attention than businesses that process most of their revenue electronically. That's a structural issue with cash businesses generally, not something specific to any one type of salon or ownership.

**Cash transactions are simply harder to verify.** When a large share of daily revenue comes in as cash, there's no automatic third-party record the way there is with card payments, which means the IRS and state tax agencies rely more heavily on a business's own bookkeeping to establish income.

**Tip reporting is a known compliance gap across the service industry.** Technicians who receive cash tips are legally required to report them as income, but enforcement is difficult, and underreporting of tips is a documented issue across nail salons, restaurants, and other tipped-service businesses alike.

**The IRS runs industry-specific compliance programs.** Rather than targeting individual businesses at random, tax agencies periodically focus enforcement resources on industries flagged as high-risk for cash underreporting — nail salons have been included in these industry-wide initiatives in some states, alongside restaurants and other cash-intensive trades.

**Good bookkeeping is the actual differentiator, not ownership type.** Salons of any ownership background that use modern point-of-sale systems, deposit cash regularly and consistently, and keep clear technician commission records face materially lower audit risk than those that don't — regardless of who owns them.

The takeaway for salon owners isn't that any particular group is being singled out — it's that cash-heavy small businesses in general benefit from tighter bookkeeping practices than businesses with mostly electronic payments.
