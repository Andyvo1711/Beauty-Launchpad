Salon owners in many parts of the country describe the same problem: more client demand than they have chairs staffed to handle. A shortage of licensed nail technicians has been building for years, and it shapes everything from wait times to prices.

**Licensing requirements vary widely by state.** In states where nail technicians must complete a full cosmetology license rather than a nail-specific one, the training hours and cost are significantly higher, which narrows the pipeline of new workers willing to invest the time.

**Turnover is high industry-wide.** The physical demands of the job — repetitive hand motion, standing or sitting for long stretches, and exposure to chemical odors — push some technicians toward other work within a few years of licensing, even when pay is competitive.

**Pay structures vary a lot by state and by salon.** Compensation models differ so much between commission-based, booth-rental, and hourly-plus-tips arrangements that comparing "average pay" across the industry is difficult, and that inconsistency makes it harder for the profession to compete for new workers against clearer-cut hourly jobs.

**Fewer new students are entering training programs.** Enrollment at many cosmetology and nail-technician schools has softened compared to a decade ago, even as the client-facing demand for services has grown.

The result is a familiar pattern in a lot of markets: fewer walk-in slots, longer waits for appointments with experienced technicians, and salons investing more in retention just to keep the chairs they already have staffed.
